3 uses of Blockchain outside finTech


For this article to make sense I need to introduce one small concept. Hash function. It’s simply a math function that will take any data/file of any size as input and give you a fixed length output. The output called a hash is meaningless and typically looks like:

                88677d15ead48ad6b825adc950ab672bd9745438a1c16480409

Ok, why ? Even if you change the original file slightly you get a totally different hash. So, this allows you to get data from unreliable sources but have the confidence that it was never tampered with (as long as you know know the hash)

This technique is pretty old and is now a standard offering for security in the developer's toolbox. Bitcoin makes use of this extensively on many levels.






Digital Art


The digital painter has problems. How do you sell your art ? How do you compete with the ‘hanging it in your drawing room for everyone to see’ that traditional art can offer ? What privileges can being an owner of any digital content offer me when it is so trivial to make copies.

Crypto has a solution for this. Take the picture (or any file) , make a hash of it and publish it on the blockchain. You are essentially proving that you are the author of the content. This technique can be wrapped in a token that allows it to use existing blockchain wallet/account infrastructure for transferring ownership of this asset. At the same time this kind of token recognises that this represents a unique asset unlike your typical token. Hence, Non Fungible Token or NFT.

This opens doors for content makers allowing them to take a get a cut everytime ownership is transferred or to sell licences directly on the blockchain.

Beeple is a renowned digital artist who had this project called “everydays”. He just wanted to force himself to make better art by posting a new piece everyday. He started around 2008 and you can really see him evolve in this series.

                              Few of the ‘everydays’ in chronological order

He has a body of other work, including commercial work for Nike and Apple, and he was involved with NTFs since their inception. Earlier this year he sold the Everydays collection NFT for 69 million. This made him the third-most-valuable living artist. 




They don't have to be just art. Currently, a bigger use case of NFT’s is actually collectables. The NBA is making collectables out of the highlights of each game under the ‘Top Shot’ brand. The most valuable collection is the cryptopunks, 10,000 uniquely generated pixel art characters that were the first NFT’s on ethereum, initially free to claim for everyone.

Gaming

Online multiplayer games always have secondary markets for accounts and in-game items. Currently, the gaming industry is very dependent on in-game item sales and this is a growing trend. Players are really attached to these game mechanics and would prefer a better sense of ownership, ability to easily trade them and letting anyone design and create new items.

The reason they were never offered these features was not technical but because the business model won't allow it. They make the most money by selling gamers items directly.

Blockchain and NFT’s offer gaming not only the technical platform for making the game item experience better, but also the business model to make it feasible.

The incentive mechanisms common in crypto are being used in gaming too. As the players and interest in a game grow the value of the in-game items does too and game developers can get sustainable revenues from smaller moves in a richer ecosystem. There are many games using this approach.




The buyers of beeple’s everydays felt the most fitting way to exhibit the collection was by building the B20 museum across 4 of these crypto based games. Here any player can come enjoy the artworks for free.



It does not have to just be open world games either, we also have stuff like, axie infinity with cryptokitties like characters and pokemon like game play, or town send which is like a competitive farmville.



Storage

Here is a short introduction to why piratebay/bittorrent was so successful:

Some pirates were good at compressing a 4GB movie DVD into 700MB, while others are good at breaking into adobe software. They cared about their reputations and believed their work was making the world a fairer place.

If they tried to distribute the pirated content from a central server it would get taken down sooner than later and it would also be too expensive to have enough bandwidth to serve all the demand. Enter Pirate bay.

Users wanting pirated content go to Pirate bay, where they don't get  the entire file but rather something called a torrent file. The torrent file contains the hashes of the original file along with some information that helps you find it. The source of the actual file is other people like you, that are all connected to the network and  trying to get the same file. The file is broken down into pieces. You ask others in the network the pieces you need and send pieces that others request.


This works great because it is hard to shutdown everyone in the p2p network. While, the hashes assure that the data is untampered.

This technology has a lot of the moving parts that were eventually used in bitcoin. But after bitcoin this idea of a distributed-file-system gained traction once again.

IMPS is one of the projects that made the most progress in this direction. It had many technical improvements over BitTorrent but the target use was much more ambitious now. They wanted the distributed network to power all of the internet.

 Any interaction on the web requires multiple requests for data packets that are typically served from a central location. IPFS uses this system where any file can be referenced by its hash. So the hash is now also the address. For example when you open a new show on Netflix you will get it from a bunch of guys in your town, who already saw it, rather than the nearest AWS server.

No amount of technical improvements can solve the ‘free-rider problem’ systems like this face. Many users of torrents would only be part of the network till they got what they needed and not contribute back. This problem can be solved by incentives, and blockchain handles those well. That is why the makers of IPFS simultaneously developed Filecoin. Now you can pay to have your data hosted.  The protocol will ensure that storage providers are well-behaved by penalising them for losing or mishandling your data and rewarding them in small intervals for proving your data is well stored.
